Turning heart-healthy science into daily habits

Thrive is an AI-powered, gamified wellness companion designed to translate evidence-based guidelines into small, sustainable actions that help people move more and live healthier.

Timeline Aug 2025 – Present
4+ months in progress
Team 3 cross-functional consultants
My Role UI Designer · UX Researcher
Focus User research · Prototyping · Journey mapping · Healthcare UX

Less than half of U.S. adults meet weekly activity guidelines, sustaining preventable disease risk.

This gap compounds existing health inequities by leaving communities without approachable guidance that fits real day-to-day routines. Sedentary habits continue not because people lack data, but because support rarely maps to their lived experience.

Problem

Tracking alone does not help people build lasting heart-healthy habits.

Most wellness apps excel at data collection but stop short of motivating sustained change. Dashboards and streaks rarely address the friction that undermines follow-through once novelty fades.

Our research confirmed that persistently low activity levels stem from entrenched behavioral barriers—not a lack of information. Users need coaching that flexes with their routines, clarifies why metrics matter, and rewards incremental wins.

Research Highlights

Behavior science and interviews revealed three persistent blockers.

We paired literature reviews and competitive analysis with interviews across community fitness programs to understand why motivation collapses.

  • REASON 01 · MOTIVATION COLLAPSES WITHOUT PERSONAL FEEDBACK Motivation drops sharply after the first two weeks when feedback feels generic.
  • REASON 02 · LOW HEALTH LITERACY BLOCKS ACTION Limited health literacy makes it hard to translate metrics into meaningful action.
  • REASON 03 · RIGID PLANS CAN'T ADAPT TO REAL LIFE Rigid plans ignore fluctuating energy, schedules, and physical limitations.

These findings reframed Thrive as more than an activity tracker; it needed to become a coach that adapts, explains, and celebrates.

Insight

Confidence grows when people see how small actions compound.

The core opportunity was to connect daily decisions with cardiovascular impact. When participants understood why a prompt mattered—and how to adjust it—they maintained momentum.

Behavior change sticks when guidance is adaptive, affirming, and framed around achievable wins instead of perfection.

Design Question

How might we translate clinical guidance into repeatable daily actions people actually want to do?

We framed every iteration against this question to prevent feature creep and keep the product grounded in behavior science.

Solution Overview

Thrive reframes movement as an adaptive, rewarding journey.

We mapped each validated barrier directly to a feature response and grounded the decision in published evidence so stakeholders could see exactly why Thrive works.

PROBLEM 01 · LACK OF MOTIVATION & SUSTAINED ENGAGEMENT Feature: Gamification & reward loops

Systematic reviews show gamified interventions drive small-to-moderate increases in physical activity (Hedges’ g ≈ 0.42) and repeatedly report significant lifts in engagement, motivation, and participation.

“Gamification significantly enhances user motivation to sustain physical activity, with meta-analyses showing meaningful effect sizes on long-term behavior change.”

PROBLEM 02 · LOW HEALTH LITERACY & UNCLEAR GUIDELINES Feature: Contextual education & movement reframing

Behavior change research confirms that goal setting, feedback, action planning, and knowledge shaping help people understand what counts as effective movement.

Transparent, plain-language visuals build self-efficacy by showing exactly what “healthy” looks like each day.

PROBLEM 03 · ONE-SIZE-FITS-ALL PLANS FAIL Feature: Personalized & adaptive plans

The Behavior Change Wheel highlights personalized action planning and adaptive content as drivers of behavior maintenance. Reviews also tie self-regulation, autonomy, and relevance to higher retention.

Tailored plans increase perceived relevance and self-regulation—two of the strongest predictors of sustained mobile health app usage.

PROBLEM 04 · TIME & LIFESTYLE CONSTRAINTS Feature: Context-aware nudges & flexible timing

Persistence studies identify logistical constraints—especially lack of time—as the main reason users abandon fitness apps. Context awareness and adaptive recommendations reduce friction and keep daily micro-actions on the record.

Flexible tracking windows and adaptive reminders honor real routines, a core principle for improving adherence over the long term.

Iteration

From novelty-driven gamification to sustained behavior change.

Version 1 — Flashy but fleeting

The initial prototype leaned on gamified streaks. Early engagement was high, yet motivation dropped as rewards felt disconnected from users’ daily realities.

Version 2 — Guided retention loops

We rebuilt the journey around reinforcement schedules, contextual tips, and social accountability. The experience now guides users through right-sized goals that adapt weekly.

Final Experience

A cohesive companion for daily heart health.

  • Personalized guidance that updates with every check-in and movement log.
  • Health overviews that connect sleep, vitals, and activity into one story.
  • Reward systems that reinforce consistent effort rather than perfection.
  • Community journeys that transform progress into a shared experience.

Each component maps directly to the research-backed barriers identified earlier, closing the loop between insight and implementation.

Deliverables

Everything packaged for stakeholder review.

High-fidelity Figma prototypes, a behavioral design style guide, and workshop-ready presentation decks are prepared for the next round of validation.

Reflection

Evidence-based design turns guidelines into lived routines.

This project reinforced that effective health products depend more on motivation, accessibility, and sustainability than on perfect data accuracy.

Grounding decisions in research—rather than assumption—showed how UX strategy can translate public health insights into daily behavior change.
